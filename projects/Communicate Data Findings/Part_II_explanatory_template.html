Part 2: Understanding Ford GoBike User Behavior¶

by Yazan Al-Sedih¶

Investigation Overview¶

This section highlights the key findings and insights discovered from the Ford GoBike trip data, aiming to understand user behavior and trip patterns across the bike-sharing network. The analysis begins with an executive summary presenting the main insights succinctly.

Key Insights¶

  • Subscribers vs. Customers: Most Subscribers take shorter and more frequent trips compared to Customers.
  • Gender Differences: There are notable differences in trip patterns between males and females, especially in terms of trip duration and frequency.
  • Temporal Patterns: Bike usage varies across days of the week and hours of the day, with some periods being busier than others.
  • Station Hubs: Certain stations emerge as major starting or ending points for trips, indicating their importance in the bike-sharing network.

Dataset Overview and Executive Summary¶

The dataset contains detailed records on Ford GoBike trips, including:

  • Trip duration
  • Start and end times
  • User type
  • Gender
  • Age
  • Start and end station locations

Summary of Findings Supported by Visualizations¶

  • Trip Duration: Varies by user type, gender, and day of the week.
  • Subscriber Behavior: Subscribers take trips more regularly than Customers.
  • Gender Patterns: Males and females show clear differences in trip duration and frequency.
  • Station Importance: Some stations act as major hubs for trip starts or ends, highlighting their network importance.

Data Cleaning and Preparation¶

Before performing detailed analysis on the Ford GoBike dataset, it is important to clean and prepare the data. The following steps are applied:

  1. Convert timestamps:
    Convert the start_time and end_time columns to datetime objects to facilitate time-based analysis.

  2. Extract time features:
    Create new columns such as start_hour, start_day_of_week, and start_month to analyze patterns by hour, day, or month.

  3. Calculate age:
    Compute the age of each user based on member_birth_year and the dataset year (2019) to study age-related usage patterns.

  4. Handle missing values:

    • Fill missing member_gender values with 'Unknown'.
    • Fill missing age values with the median age to avoid skewing analyses.

  5. Remove unrealistic or invalid data:

    • Remove trips with non-positive duration (duration_sec <= 0).
    • Filter out users with unrealistic ages (less than 10 or greater than 100).

  6. Reset index:
    After filtering rows, reset the DataFrame index for a clean dataset.

  7. Quick inspection:
    Print the info and first few rows to confirm that the data is cleaned and ready for analysis.

  8. Save cleaned data:
    Export the cleaned dataset to a new CSV file for future use.

# Data Cleaning and Preparation

# 1. Convert start_time and end_time to datetime
df['start_time'] = pd.to_datetime(df['start_time'])
df['end_time'] = pd.to_datetime(df['end_time'])

# 2. Extract useful time features for analysis
df['start_hour'] = df['start_time'].dt.hour
df['start_day_of_week'] = df['start_time'].dt.day_name()
df['start_month'] = df['start_time'].dt.month

# 3. Calculate age based on birth year
current_year = 2019  # since dataset is Feb 2019
df['age'] = current_year - df['member_birth_year']

# 4. Handle missing or null values
df['member_gender'] = df['member_gender'].fillna('Unknown')  # Fill missing gender
median_age = df['age'].median()
df['age'] = df['age'].fillna(median_age)  # Fill missing ages with median

# 5. Remove unrealistic or invalid data
df = df[df['duration_sec'] > 0]  # Remove trips with non-positive duration
df = df[(df['age'] >= 10) & (df['age'] <= 100)]  # Filter unrealistic ages

# 6. Optional: reset index after filtering
df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)

# 7. Quick check of cleaned data
print(df.info())
print(df.head())

# 8. Save cleaned dataframe to a new CSV file for future use
df.to_csv("fordgobike_tripdata_cleaned.csv", index=False)

Observations:¶

  • Subscribers dominate usage: With 163,477 trips (89.2%), subscribers account for nearly 9 out of every 10 trips in the system.

  • Customer usage is minimal: Customers made only 19,863 trips (10.8%), suggesting the service is primarily used by regular commuters rather than casual or tourist riders.

  • Business implication: The heavy reliance on subscribers indicates a stable, predictable revenue stream and suggests that marketing efforts should focus on converting occasional users into subscribers.

  • Insight: This 89-11 split suggests Ford GoBike has successfully built a loyal subscriber base, but there's untapped potential in the customer segment that could be explored through targeted campaigns.

Visualization 2: Trip Duration by User Type¶

This box plot compares the distribution of trip durations (in minutes) between Customers and Subscribers, focusing on the typical range of ride lengths. To improve clarity, the y-axis is limited to 120 minutes, which covers the majority of trips and highlights the most meaningful differences between user groups. Extreme outliers above this threshold are rare and excluded for visualization purposes but are acknowledged in the discussion.

Observations:¶

  • Customers tend to have longer trip durations on average compared to Subscribers (with the median for Customers around 13 minutes and for Subscribers around 8 minutes).

  • Subscribers show a more compact, focused usage pattern, mainly for short, quick commutes; Customers' usage is more variable and includes more leisure-oriented, long rides.

  • Both groups have outliers, but extreme values above 120 minutes are rare and likely represent unreturned bikes or data anomalies.

  • Limiting the y-axis to 120 minutes visually emphasizes typical behavior without being distorted by a handful of very long trips.

Observations:¶

  • Customers—especially older members—take longer trips on average and show greater variability in trip duration compared to Subscribers.

  • Subscribers are most common in younger age groups, with trip durations generally short and tightly clustered, reflecting regular commuting patterns.

  • There is no clear gender-based difference in trip duration; all marker styles are widely distributed and overlap for both user types.

  • Most long trips (especially above 60 minutes) are concentrated among older Customers, while shorter trips (mostly below 30 minutes) are common among younger Subscribers.

  • Both age and user type strongly influence trip patterns, while gender, in this dataset, appears to play only a minimal role in determining trip length.
